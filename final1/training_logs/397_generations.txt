###### ORIGINAL PROMPT #####

Ten friends decide to get an end-of-year gift for their teacher. They plan to split the cost of the gift equally. But four of the group drop out. The remaining friends split the cost equally among themselves. If each share is now $8 more, how much does the gift cost, in dollars?

#### ANS ####

120
#### GENERATION 1 RESPONSE ####


system
Let the total cost of the gift be \( C \) dollars. Initially, 10 friends were planning to split the cost equally, so each friend's share would be \( \frac{C}{10} \). When 4 friends drop out, only 6 friends remain to split the cost, so each friend's new share is \( \frac{C}{6} \). According to the problem, each share is now $8 more expensive, so we can set up the following equation:

\[
\frac{C}{6} = \frac{C}{10} + 8
\]

To solve for \( C \), we first eliminate the fractions by finding a common denominator. The least common multiple of 6 and 10 is 30, so we multiply every term by 30:

\[
30 \cdot \frac{C}{6} = 30 \cdot \frac{C}{10} + 30 \cdot 8
\]

Simplifying, we get:

\[
5C = 3C + 240
\]

Subtracting \( 3C \) from both sides, we obtain:

\[
2C = 240
\]

Dividing both sides by 2, we find:

\[
C = 120
\]

So, the cost of the gift is \( \boxed{120} \) dollars.
